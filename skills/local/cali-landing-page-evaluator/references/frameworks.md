# Landing page frameworks by category

Five categories of product/service/brand, each with a base framework, when to
use it, and how to avoid robotic/generic tone specifically within that
category.

Each category has: **structural framework**, **what to prioritize**,
**category-specific pitfalls** (not the generic ones — those live in
anti-ai-tone.md), and an **example section skeleton**.

---

## 1. SaaS / technical tool (rational decision + trust)

**When to use:** software product, tool, API, technical product where the
visitor already roughly knows what they're looking for and will evaluate
comparatively.

**Base framework:** Honest PAS (Problem named with precision → Agitation
that's real, not inflated → concrete Solution) combined with FAB
(Feature → Advantage → Benefit) for the features section.

**Prioritize:**
- Proof/demo moved to the first fold (screenshot, GIF, real number), not
  buried at the bottom.
- Headline naming the problem or the outcome with specificity — never the
  generic product category.
- Every feature explicitly tied to an outcome, not listed as a spec sheet.

**Category pitfalls:**
- Headline that describes the category ("The project management platform")
  instead of the outcome ("Stop missing deadlines because of a stale
  spreadsheet").
- Feature list with no context for why it matters.
- Generic social proof ("10,000+ companies trust us") with no name, no
  context, no specific outcome number.

**Section skeleton:**
1. Headline = named problem or specific outcome
2. Sub-headline = who you are / what you do in one jargon-free sentence
3. Immediate proof (screenshot, real metric, or named testimonial excerpt)
4. Problem → Agitation (real, short) → Solution
5. Features as Feature→Advantage→Benefit (3-5, not more)
6. Anticipated objections (price, learning curve, migration)
7. Single, specific CTA that says what happens after clicking

## 2. High-involvement relational service (coaching, consulting, B2B mentorship)

**When to use:** 1:1 or small-group services, purchase decision is
emotional + rational, the client is evaluating whether to trust the
person/process, not just the outcome.

**Base framework:** StoryBrand (the client is the hero of the story, you
are the guide who helps — not the protagonist) combined with BAB
(Before → After → Bridge).

**Prioritize:**
- Name the problem the way the client would name it (their language, not
  your field's jargon).
- Position yourself as a guide with authority + empathy — not a savior, not
  a salesperson.
- A real transformation story (from a specific client, with context), not
  an abstract "amazing results."

**Category pitfalls:**
- Making yourself/your brand the hero of the story ("After 15 years helping
  hundreds of companies transform...").
- Promising generic transformation without showing the specific "before."
- High-friction CTA right away (buy now) when the offer requires more
  trust first — consider a lower-friction intermediate CTA (call,
  diagnostic, conversation).

**Section skeleton:**
1. Headline = the outcome the client wants, in their own language
2. The problem as the client lives it (Before, specific)
3. Why it's stayed unsolved (what they've already tried that didn't work)
4. You as guide: brief credential + genuine empathy (one "I understand
   because..." sentence)
5. How the process/plan works (simple, concrete steps, not vague)
6. Proof: 1-2 real stories with context (not a loose quote)
7. Invitation CTA, not pressure — "let's talk" over "buy now"

## 3. Therapeutic practice / personal care / vulnerable audience

**When to use:** therapy, mental health, care-based practices, any context
where the visitor may be in a vulnerable or overwhelmed moment.

**Base framework:** this isn't "selling" in the classic sense. It's
**clarity + safety**. An orientation structure, not aggressive persuasion.

**Prioritize:**
- Headline as the question the visitor would ask out loud or type into
  Google ("Can therapy help with excessive self-criticism?") instead of a
  slogan.
- Plain language alongside the technical term, never the technical term
  alone.
- Few CTAs (1 to 3) — too many CTAs creates decision fatigue for someone
  who's already overwhelmed.
- No artificial urgency ("limited spots!") — it breaks the sense of safety.

**Category pitfalls:**
- Tone that's too clinical and cold (loses connection) or too salesy
  (breaks trust).
- Promising a categorical result/cure.
- High-pressure CTA ("book now!!") instead of a calm invitation.

**Section skeleton:**
1. Headline = the question the visitor is already asking themselves
2. Brief validation of what they're feeling, without dramatizing
3. How the process works, in plain language with clear steps
4. About the practitioner: credential + genuine humanity (one personal
   sentence)
5. What to expect from the first conversation/session (reduces
   uncertainty = reduces the barrier)
6. Single CTA, invitation tone ("if this feels right, let's talk")

## 4. Physical product / experience / e-commerce / lifestyle

**When to use:** physical product, experience (trip, retreat, event),
decision is more sensory/impulsive.

**Base framework:** Sensory AIDA — energy, imagery, shorter sentence
rhythm, visual storytelling driving the decision.

**Prioritize:**
- Active, sensory language (action verbs, concrete images).
- Varied and shorter sentence rhythm than the other categories.
- Real scarcity when it genuinely exists, never fabricated.

**Category pitfalls:**
- Empty adjective overload ("amazing," "life-changing") with no concrete
  image behind it.
- Generic storytelling that could belong to any brand in the niche.

**Section skeleton:**
1. Headline = specific image/moment that triggers desire (Attention)
2. Sensory detail that connects to the reader's life (Interest)
3. Building desire with specificity (Desire) — not "unique experience,"
   but exactly what happens
4. Clear action CTA, with risk-reversal reinforcement if applicable
   (guarantee, easy cancellation)

## 5. Open-source project / developer tool (peer-to-peer)

**When to use:** open-source project, technical tool for other developers,
where the "customer" is a skeptical technical peer, not a marketing lead.

**Base framework:** good-README structure — **Problem → Approach →
Evidence (cited research/data) → How to try it**. No sales rhetoric;
persuasion comes from technical honesty and transparent trade-offs.

**Prioritize:**
- First person, explicitly acknowledging limitations and trade-offs.
- Real evidence (benchmark, cited paper, justified architecture decision)
  instead of adjectives.
- Frictionless "try it" path (install command, repo link) instead of lead
  capture.

**Category pitfalls:**
- Any "revolutionary," "next-gen," "the future of X" language — developers
  spot it instantly and discount it.
- Promising without showing the technical decision behind it.
- Email/lead-capture CTA instead of "see the code" / "run it yourself."

**Section skeleton:**
1. Headline = what the project does, hype-free, in one clear technical
   sentence
2. The problem that motivated the project (real context, not generic pain)
3. Approach/architecture decisions, with trade-offs acknowledged
4. Evidence: benchmark, comparison, or cited research
5. How to try it (command, link, quickstart) — minimal friction
6. Known limitations / honest roadmap
