# Anti-AI-tone / anti-marketing-bullshit checklist

Apply this to every evaluation and every piece of generated copy, regardless
of category.

## Vocabulary to flag (immediate red flag)
"elevate", "unlock", "seamless", "journey", "unleash", "revolutionary",
"empower", "leverage" (as a generic verb), "cutting-edge", "next-level",
"game-changer", "world-class", "best-in-class", "supercharge", "one-stop
solution", "industry-leading" without proof, "discover the power of",
"transform your life/business".

If one of these shows up, flag it as something to rewrite — not an absolute
ban (it can occasionally fit), but a warning sign that needs justification.

## Specificity tests
- Does every benefit claim have a real number, example, or name behind it?
  If not, either add specificity or cut the sentence.
- Does social proof have context (who, when, what specifically changed)?
  "10,000+ happy customers" fails this test. "Ana, who was juggling 3
  parallel spreadsheets, got down to 1 dashboard in 2 weeks" passes.
- Does each section survive the test "could this sentence sit on any
  competitor's page in this niche"? If yes, it's too generic.

## Rhythm and voice tests
- A whole paragraph of same-length sentences is a tell for generated text.
  Vary sentence length deliberately.
- First person where it fits (a real person is writing this, not "our
  team" or a corporate "we," unless the brand genuinely is a large team).
- Contractions and natural speech imperfections beat artificial formality
  (calibrate by category — a therapeutic practice tolerates less
  informality than a SaaS product).

## CTA and pressure tests
- Is there only 1 central CTA per page (or at most 2-3 if the category
  calls for it, like therapy)?
- Does the CTA say what happens after clicking, not just a generic verb
  ("Get started" with no context)?
- Scarcity/urgency: is it real? If it can't be confirmed as real, treat it
  as a red flag.

## Structural test
- Does the page have a single central promise, or is it trying to convince
  the visitor of 3 different things at once?
- Does the chosen framework actually match the category (see
  frameworks.md), or was it applied generically without considering the
  type of decision the visitor is making (rational vs. emotional vs.
  technical-skeptical)?
